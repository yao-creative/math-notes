Your intent is **formalizing categorical constructions as explicit set-builder predicates**. The key distinction is:

1. **Morphisms**: elements of hom-sets.
2. **Natural transformations**: families of morphisms satisfying a commutativity predicate.
3. **Generators**: elements whose closure under allowed operations generates an object.
4. **Universal properties**: existence-and-uniqueness predicates over morphisms.

## 1. Morphisms in set-builder notation

A category can be represented as:

$$
\mathcal C
=
\left(
\operatorname{Ob}(\mathcal C),
\operatorname{Mor}(\mathcal C),
s,t,\circ,\operatorname{id}
\right)
$$

where the morphisms are partitioned by source and target:

$$
\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal C}(A,B)
=
\{f\in\operatorname{Mor}(\mathcal C)
\mid s(f)=A\land t(f)=B\}.
$$

Thus a morphism is simply:

$$
f:A\to B
\iff
f\in\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal C}(A,B).
$$

In **Set**:

$$
\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbf{Set}}(A,B)
=
\{f\subseteq A\times B
\mid
\forall a\in A;\exists!b\in B:(a,b)\in f
\}.
$$

So a function is a set of ordered pairs satisfying the totality and uniqueness predicate:

$$
f:A\to B
\iff
f\subseteq A\times B
\land
\forall a\in A;\exists!b\in B:(a,b)\in f.
$$

Composition is then:

$$
g\circ f
=
\{(a,c)\in A\times C
\mid
\exists b\in B:
(a,b)\in f\land(b,c)\in g
\}.
$$

# 2. Natural morphisms: natural transformations

Strictly, the natural object is usually called a **natural transformation**, not a "natural morphism".

Given functors

$$
F,G:\mathcal C\to\mathcal D,
$$

a natural transformation

$$
\eta:F\Rightarrow G
$$

is the set of component morphisms:

$$
\eta
=
\{(A,\eta_A)
\mid
A\in\operatorname{Ob}(\mathcal C)
\land
\eta_A\in
\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal D}(F(A),G(A))
\}.
$$

But that family is a **natural transformation** only if it satisfies the naturality predicate:

$$
\forall
f\in
\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal C}(A,B):
\qquad
G(f)\circ\eta_A
=
\eta_B\circ F(f).
$$

So the full set-builder definition is:

$$
\operatorname{Nat}(F,G)
=
\left\{
\eta
;\middle|;
\begin{array}{l}
\eta=
\{(A,\eta_A)\mid
A\in\operatorname{Ob}(\mathcal C)\},[2mm]
\forall A:
\eta_A:F(A)\to G(A),[1mm]
\forall f:A\to B:
G(f)\circ\eta_A
=
\eta_B\circ F(f)
\end{array}
\right\}.
$$

The important conceptual structure is:

$$
\boxed{
\text{family of local morphisms}
+
\text{global coherence predicate}
\;\Longrightarrow\;
\text{natural transformation}
}
$$

The naturality equation says that two different routes through the diagram are equal:

$$
F(A)
\xrightarrow{\eta_A}
G(A)
\xrightarrow{G(f)}
G(B)
$$

and

$$
F(A)
\xrightarrow{F(f)}
F(B)
\xrightarrow{\eta_B}
G(B).
$$

So naturality is fundamentally a **commutation constraint over all morphisms**.

# 3. Generators in set-builder notation

Suppose an algebraic structure is generated by a subset

$$
S\subseteq A.
$$

The substructure generated by $S$ is the intersection of all substructures containing $S$:

$$
\langle S\rangle
=
\bigcap
\{B\subseteq A
\mid
B\text{ is a substructure of }A
\land
S\subseteq B
\}.
$$

In pure set-builder style:

$$
\langle S\rangle
=
\bigcap
\left\{
B\subseteq A
;\middle|;
S\subseteq B
\land
\operatorname{Closed}_{\Omega}(B)
\right\}.
$$

Here $\Omega$ is the collection of operations of the algebra.

For a monoid $(M,\cdot,e)$:

$$
\operatorname{Closed}(B)
\iff
e\in B
\land
\forall x,y\in B:
x\cdot y\in B.
$$

Therefore:

$$
\langle S\rangle
=
\bigcap
\left\{
B\subseteq M
;\middle|;
S\subseteq B
\land
e\in B
\land
\forall x,y\in B:
x,y\in B\Rightarrow x\cdot y\in B
\right\}.
$$

Equivalently, for a monoid, the generated submonoid is:

$$
\langle S\rangle
=
\left\{
s_1s_2\cdots s_n
;\middle|;
n\geq 0
\land
\forall i\in\{1,\ldots,n\}:s_i\in S
\right\}.
$$

The $n=0$ case gives the identity:

$$
s_1\cdots s_0=e.
$$

For a single generator $g$:

$$
\langle g\rangle
=
\{g^n\mid n\in\mathbb N\}.
$$

This is a **monogenic monoid**.

For a group:

$$
\langle g\rangle
=
\{g^n\mid n\in\mathbb Z\}.
$$

The essential difference is:

$$
\boxed{
\text{generator}
\;\Longrightarrow\;
\text{element(s) whose closure equals the target structure}
}
$$

Formally:

$$
S\text{ generates }A
\iff
\langle S\rangle=A.
$$

# 4. Universal property in set-builder notation

A universal property is fundamentally an **existence-and-uniqueness predicate over morphisms**.

Suppose $U$ is intended to be a product of $A$ and $B$, with projections:

$$
\pi_A:U\to A,
\qquad
\pi_B:U\to B.
$$

The universal property is:

$$
\forall X\in\operatorname{Ob}(\mathcal C)
;\forall f:X\to A
;\forall g:X\to B:
\exists!h:X\to U
\quad
\text{s.t.}
$$

$$
\pi_A\circ h=f
\land
\pi_B\circ h=g.
$$

In pure set-builder form:

$$
\operatorname{Product}(U,A,B)
\iff
\exists\pi_A,\pi_B:
\left[
\begin{array}{l}
\pi_A\in\operatorname{Hom}(U,A),\ \pi_B\in\operatorname{Hom}(U,B),\\[1mm]
\forall X,f,g:\ \left(f\in\operatorname{Hom}(X,A)\land g\in\operatorname{Hom}(X,B)\right)\Rightarrow\\
\exists!h\in\operatorname{Hom}(X,U):\ \pi_A\circ h=f\land\pi_B\circ h=g
\end{array}
\right].
$$

The $\exists!$ is crucial. It means:

$$
\exists h
\quad\land\quad
\forall h_1,h_2:
\left[
\operatorname{Valid}(h_1)
\land
\operatorname{Valid}(h_2)
\Rightarrow
h_1=h_2
\right].
$$

So the universal property decomposes into:

$$
\boxed{
\text{existence}
+
\text{uniqueness}
+
\text{commuting equations}
}
$$

## The three constructions side by side

| Concept                | Set-builder structure                                                     |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Morphism               | $f\in\operatorname{Hom}(A,B)$                                             |
| Natural transformation | ${\eta_A}_{A\in\mathcal C}$ satisfying $\forall f,;G(f)\eta_A=\eta_BF(f)$ |
| Generator              | $S\subseteq A$ such that $\langle S\rangle=A$                             |
| Universal property     | $\forall$ compatible data, $\exists!$ morphism satisfying equations       |

The deepest distinction is:

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\text{Morphisms} &: \text{allowed arrows} \\
\text{Natural transformations} &: \text{coherent families of arrows} \\
\text{Generators} &: \text{minimal data whose closure produces an object} \\
\text{Universal properties} &: \text{unique arrows induced by arbitrary compatible data}
\end{aligned}
}
$$

There is also a useful duality between the last two:

$$
\text{Generators}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{closure under operations}
$$

whereas

$$
\text{Universal property}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{unique extension of maps}.
$$

In category theory, this is why a free object is simultaneously:

$$
\boxed{
\text{generated by formal generators}
\quad+\quad
\text{universal among all realizations of those generators}
}
$$

For example, the free monoid on a set $X$ is:

$$
X^*
=
\{(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\mid n\in\mathbb N,\;x_i\in X\},
$$

with concatenation. Its universal property is:

$$
\forall M\in\mathbf{Monoid}
;\forall f:X\to U(M):
\exists! \bar f:X^*\to M
$$

such that:

$$
\bar f([x])=f(x).
$$

The function $\bar f$ is the unique **homomorphic extension** of the generator assignment $f$.
