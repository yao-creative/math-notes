Let's rebuild the whole chain from first principles.

The intent here is to understand **why the sign map exists categorically**. The key objects are:

$$
S_n,\quad A_n,\quad S_n/A_n,\quad C_2,\quad S_n^{ab}
$$

---

# 1. What is $S_n$?

$S_n$ is the **symmetric group** on $n$ elements.

It is the group of all permutations of:

$$
{1,2,\dots,n}
$$

A permutation is a bijection:

$$
\sigma:{1,\dots,n}\rightarrow{1,\dots,n}
$$

The group operation is composition:

$$
(\sigma\circ\tau)(x)=\sigma(\tau(x))
$$

Example:

$$
S_3
$$

contains all rearrangements of:

$$
{1,2,3}
$$

There are:

$$
3!=6
$$

elements.

They are:

$$
\begin{aligned}
e&=(1)(2)(3)\\
(12)&\\
(13)&\\
(23)&\\
(123)&\\
(132)&
\end{aligned}
$$

The notation:

$$
(123)
$$

means:

$$
1\rightarrow2,\quad2\rightarrow3,\quad3\rightarrow1
$$

---

# 2. What is $A_n$?

Every permutation can be written as a product of swaps (transpositions):

$$
(a\ b)
$$

Example:

$$
(123)=(13)(12)
$$

A transposition is an odd permutation because it takes one swap.

Define:

$$
\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)=(-1)^k
$$

where $k$ is the number of swaps.

So:

* even number of swaps:

$$
\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)=+1
$$

* odd number of swaps:

$$
\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)=-1
$$

The **alternating group** is:

$$
\boxed{
A_n={\sigma\in S_n\mid \sigma\text{ is even}}
}
$$

It is the subgroup of all even permutations.

---

Example:

For:

$$
S_3
$$

we have:

$$
S_3=
{
e,(12),(13),(23),(123),(132)
}
$$

Even permutations:

$$
e,(123),(132)
$$

Therefore:

$$
\boxed{
A_3={e,(123),(132)}
}
$$

and:

$$
|A_3|=3
$$

while:

$$
|S_3|=6
$$

---

# 3. What is a quotient group?

Now:

$$
S_n/A_n
$$

means:

"Collapse all elements of $A_n$ into one object."

The formal idea:

Two elements are equivalent if they differ by an element of $A_n$:

$$
\sigma\sim\tau
$$

when:

$$
\sigma^{-1}\tau\in A_n
$$

The equivalence classes are called **cosets**.

---

For $S_3/A_3$:

The subgroup:

$$
A_3={e,(123),(132)}
$$

creates two buckets.

### Bucket 1: even permutations

$$
A_3
$$

itself:

$$
{e,(123),(132)}
$$

---

### Bucket 2: odd permutations

Pick:

$$
(12)
$$

Multiply it by every element of $A_3$:

$$
(12)A_3
$$

giving:

$$
{
(12),
(12)(123),
(12)(132)
}
$$

which becomes:

$$
{
(12),(23),(13)
}
$$

So:

$$
S_3/A_3
=======

{
A_3,(12)A_3
}
$$

There are exactly two elements.

---

# 4. What is $C_2$?

$C_2$ means the **cyclic group of order 2**.

It is:

$$
\boxed{
C_2={0,1}
}
$$

with addition modulo 2.

The operation table:

$$
\begin{array}{c|cc}
+&0&1\\
\hline
0&0&1\\
1&1&0
\end{array}
$$

or multiplicatively:

$$
\boxed{
C_2={+1,-1}
}
$$

with:

$$
(-1)(-1)=+1
$$

---

The quotient:

$$
S_n/A_n
$$

only remembers:

"was the permutation even or odd?"

Therefore it has exactly two states:

$$
\begin{cases}
\text{even}\\
\text{odd}
\end{cases}
$$

which corresponds to:

$$
\begin{cases}
+1\\
-1
\end{cases}
$$

Hence:

$$
\boxed{
S_n/A_n\cong C_2
}
$$

The isomorphism is:

$$
[\sigma]\mapsto \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)
$$

---

# 5. Why is $A_n=[S_n,S_n]$?

The commutator:

$$
[\sigma,\tau]
=============

\sigma\tau\sigma^{-1}\tau^{-1}
$$

measures failure of commuting.

The commutator subgroup:

$$
[S_n,S_n]
$$

is all possible products of these failures.

For $S_n$:

$$
\boxed{
[S_n,S_n]=A_n
}
$$

Intuition:

* commutators are always even
* every even permutation can be generated by commutators

Therefore the "non-commutative part" of $S_n$ is exactly $A_n$.

---

# 6. Therefore the abelianization

By definition:

$$
G^{ab}=G/[G,G]
$$

For:

$$
G=S_n
$$

we get:

$$
S_n^{ab}
========

S_n/[S_n,S_n]
$$

Substitute:

$$
[S_n,S_n]=A_n
$$

so:

$$
\boxed{
S_n^{ab}=S_n/A_n
}
$$

and since:

$$
S_n/A_n\cong C_2
$$

we get:

$$
\boxed{
S_n^{ab}\cong C_2
}
$$

---

# 7. Universal property

Now the category theory.

The abelianization is not just a quotient. It is the **best possible abelian approximation**.

We have:

$$
\pi:S_n\rightarrow S_n^{ab}
$$

where:

$$
\pi(\sigma)=[\sigma]
$$

Any map from $S_n$ into an abelian group:

$$
f:S_n\rightarrow B
$$

must satisfy:

$$
f(\sigma\tau)=f(\tau\sigma)
$$

because $B$ is abelian.

Therefore:

$$
f([\sigma,\tau])=e
$$

meaning:

$$
[S_n,S_n]\subseteq\ker(f)
$$

Since:

$$
[S_n,S_n]=A_n
$$

we have:

$$
A_n\subseteq\ker(f)
$$

Therefore $f$ cannot distinguish anything inside $A_n$.

It must factor:

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
S_n&\xrightarrow{f}&B\\
\downarrow{\pi}&&\\
S_n/A_n&\xrightarrow{\bar f}&B
\end{array}
$$

Meaning:

$$
\boxed{
f=\bar f\circ\pi
}
$$

and $\bar f$ is unique.

---

## The compressed categorical statement

The inclusion functor:

$$
I:\mathbf{Ab}\rightarrow\mathbf{Grp}
$$

has a left adjoint:

$$
(-)^{ab}:\mathbf{Grp}\rightarrow\mathbf{Ab}
$$

with:

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Ab}}(G^{ab},A)
\cong
\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Grp}}(G,A)
}
$$

where $A$ is abelian.

For $S_n$:

$$
\boxed{
S_n
\rightarrow
S_n/A_n
\cong C_2
}
$$

is the universal way to extract the **only information about permutations visible to an abelian observer**:

$$
\boxed{\text{even vs odd}}
$$

That universal projection is exactly the sign homomorphism.
